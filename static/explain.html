ADA Project — Algorithm Design & Analysis

Dynamic scheduling for 14 buses across five Indore routes

Acropolis Institute's morning shift target is 08:10. Students and faculty board buses across 111 non-terminal stops on five connected routes. This deterministic reference scenario generates 428 passengers and compares three scheduling strategies under the same demand pattern.

The problem

Uneven demand at peak time

Between 07:00–07:45, boarding demand at junction stops surges 5–8x above baseline. A single overloaded bus strands 30–40 passengers while a neighbouring route's bus runs half-empty. The root cause is a pure resource-to-demand mismatch across routes.

Three routes share one junction

Routes R15, R16, and R18 converge at Dakachya — a 3-way junction. Without coordination, all three compete for the same passengers. A bus serving Dakachya first drains its capacity before the next stop's larger crowd can board.

Reassignment has a cascade cost

Moving a bus from one route to another removes it from the original route. Any scheduler that makes purely local decisions creates shortfalls downstream. The algorithm needs to weigh gain vs. loss across the entire network, not just the overloaded stop.

Target arrival: 08:10

The morning shift target is 08:10. The simulation retains a 60-minute grace window so the final buses can complete their route and every passenger outcome is recorded.

Three algorithms compared

Greedy Scheduler
Recommended

Every minute, find the most overloaded route. Scan adjacent routes for a bus at a shared junction. Reassign only if the downstream demand gain exceeds +10 passengers. Apply a 30-minute cooldown to prevent flapping.

  • Junction-aware demand comparison
  • Excludes Acropolis terminal (false positive)
  • 30-min cooldown per bus
  • Practical for larger fleet sizes

Dynamic Programming
Optimal, not scalable

Uses memoized search to compare candidate bus-to-route assignments for the current tick. It evaluates alternatives globally for the current state, but its state space grows rapidly as more candidates and routes are added.

  • Global candidate assignment per tick
  • Does not look ahead across ticks
  • Memoized state search
  • Higher cost as candidate count grows

Current real-world system

Buses follow pre-assigned routes with no dynamic reallocation. Passengers are picked up FIFO at each stop. This is the system as it currently operates at Acropolis. No coordination between routes.

  • Simple and deterministic
  • No inter-route awareness
  • Load imbalance at junctions

How the Greedy Scheduler works

The scheduler runs once per simulated minute. Here is exactly what it does:

01

Measure demand deficit per route

Compute deficit = passengers_waiting − available_seats for every route. Routes with a positive deficit are overloaded and flagged. Routes with spare capacity are candidate donors.

02

Find a bus at a shared junction

For each overloaded route, scan all active buses on other routes. A bus qualifies as a candidate only if its next scheduled stop is a shared stop — one that also appears on the overloaded route. The Acropolis terminal is explicitly excluded: buses there are done, not available.

03

Compare downstream demand before reassigning

Before committing, calculate downstream_demand_new (passengers the bus would serve on the overloaded route from the junction onward) vs downstream_demand_old (staying on its current route). Only reassign if the net gain is more than 10 passengers. This threshold prevents micro-moves that help nothing.

04

Reassign and apply cooldown

The bus's route reference is updated to the overloaded route. Its stop index is set to the junction stop it is already heading to — so it continues naturally without teleporting. A 30-minute cooldown prevents the same bus from being reassigned again too soon.

05

Observable in the simulation

Reassigned buses are shown in amber on the map. A notification bar at the top of the simulation view identifies exactly which bus was diverted and between which routes. A pulsing ring marks the diversion point on the map. You can watch the stranded count drop in real time.

Inter-Bus Communication (COMM)

Predicting downstream capacity

Buses are no longer completely greedy at shared junctions. When a bus arrives at a stop (e.g. Van Mandal), it calculates how many passengers it needs for its own exclusive downstream stops.

Looking ahead on the schedule

If the bus needs seats, it scans the network to see if another bus is scheduled to arrive at this current stop later (from any route). If a trailing bus has capacity, the current bus will intentionally "leave" students behind.

Observable [COMM] action

This appears in the dispatch log as [COMM] Bus X reserved Y seats for downstream, leaving students for Bus Z. By leaving these students for the trailing bus, the first bus guarantees it will have room for its own unique downstream passengers.

Last Resort Overload

Buses operate strictly at a 45-passenger limit, but are allowed a 3-passenger overload buffer. If a small group (<= 3) is stranded and no trailing bus is coming, the bus will utilize its overload buffer up to the hard maximum of 48 to ensure no one is left behind.

Why Greedy outperforms DP for this problem

Speed matters

The scheduler runs once per simulated minute. Greedy performs direct route and bus scans, while DP stores intermediate candidate-assignment states. This makes Greedy the safer choice as the fleet and junction network grow.

Comparable reference outcome

In the current deterministic reference run, Greedy transports 410 passengers and leaves 18 stranded, compared with 366 transported and 62 stranded for Baseline. It does this by moving one truly spare bus before departure; every boarded passenger remains within the 45-seat bus capacity.

The insight is junction awareness

The real performance gain is not Greedy vs DP — it is junction-aware scheduling vs none. Routes R15, R16, and R18 all cross at Dakachya. Without tracking shared stops, no algorithm can intervene correctly. The 3-way overlap is the bottleneck, not the assignment method.

Conservative fallback

If no eligible reallocation is found, the Greedy scheduler leaves every bus on its assigned route. This preserves normal baseline movement while avoiding low-value diversions.
